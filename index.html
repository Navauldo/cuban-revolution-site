Chapter I

Cuba Under Batista

Cuba in the 1950s was marked by stark inequality, political repression, and a deep dependency on the United States. Fulgencio Batista, who seized power in a coup in March 1952, presided over a regime that served the interests of the Cuban elite and American business interests while the majority of Cubans — particularly rural workers and Afro-Cubans — lived in poverty.

Batista's government was notorious for its corruption and brutal suppression of dissent through the secret police. Land ownership was concentrated in the hands of a few, and American corporations dominated key industries such as sugar and mining. This context of inequality and foreign domination created the conditions for revolutionary sentiment to take root.

"Cuba was a country economically dependent, politically dominated, and socially stratified — the ideal ground for revolution." — Hugh Thomas, Cuba: A History (2010)

Chapter II

The Revolutionary Struggle

Sierra Maestra
⭐
Che Guevara — Guerrillero Heroico, the most reproduced photograph in history. Taken by Alberto Korda in Havana, 1960. Alberto Korda / Wikimedia Commons · Public Domain in most jurisdictions

The revolutionary struggle began on 26 July 1953, when Fidel Castro led approximately 160 rebels in an assault on the Moncada Barracks in Santiago de Cuba. The attack failed militarily, and many rebels were killed or captured. Castro was tried and delivered his famous defence — "History Will Absolve Me" — before being sentenced to prison.

Released under amnesty in 1955, Castro regrouped in Mexico where he organised the 26th of July Movement and trained a guerrilla force. In December 1956, Castro and eighty-one rebels — including Argentine doctor Ernesto "Che" Guevara — landed in eastern Cuba aboard the yacht Granma. After a disastrous initial encounter, the survivors retreated to the Sierra Maestra mountains and launched a sustained guerrilla campaign that would last two years, gaining popular support and steadily expanding territory.

"History Will Absolve Me." — Fidel Castro, Trial Defence Speech, Santiago de Cuba, 1953

Chapter III

Triumph of the Revolution

By late 1958, momentum had decisively shifted. Batista's army was demoralised, and in a series of decisive victories, Castro's forces captured key towns across Cuba. On 31 December 1958, Batista fled the country. On 1 January 1959, Fidel Castro entered Havana triumphant.

The new government moved swiftly — land reform programmes redistributed large estates, literacy campaigns dramatically reduced illiteracy, and healthcare was extended to rural areas. Cuba's alignment with the Soviet Union set the stage for the Bay of Pigs invasion (1961) and the Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), cementing the revolution's global significance and deepening the Cold War's grip on the Caribbean.

"The revolution is not just Cuba's — it belongs to the oppressed peoples of the world." — Fidel Castro, Havana, 1960

Chapter V

Cuban Cultural Diplomacy

Beyond direct political influence, Cuba projected its revolutionary ideals across the Caribbean through cultural diplomacy. Cuban music — particularly son, salsa, and nueva trova — carried themes of social justice and anti-imperialism that resonated deeply with Caribbean audiences. The state-run ICAIC produced politically engaged films screened across the region.

Perhaps most significantly, Cuba's medical internationalism — dispatching doctors and healthcare workers to other nations — built considerable goodwill. Scholarships for Caribbean students to study in Cuban universities deepened ties and spread revolutionary ideas, presenting an alternative model of international relations based on solidarity rather than commercial interest.

"Cuba's internationalism was never merely political — it was a model of human solidarity that contrasted sharply with the perceived self-interest of larger powers." — Brian Meeks, Caribbean Revolutions and Revolutionary Theory (1993)
